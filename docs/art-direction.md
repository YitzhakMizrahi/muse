# Art Direction

This document defines the first serious visual system for `Muse`.

## Creative Direction

`Editorial Atelier`

Muse should feel like a high-end creative strategy studio: thoughtful, tactile, and composed, with AI acting as a sharp partner rather than a glowing machine.

## Visual Intent

- Premium and authored rather than template-driven
- Clear and legible before it becomes atmospheric
- Editorial and spatial rather than dashboard-like
- Human-guided, with visible transformation from raw idea to shaped direction
- Distinct without becoming niche or design-insider in tone

## Product Readability

The interface should explain itself in seconds:

1. you bring an idea
2. Muse gives you three directions
3. you choose one and refine it

If the visual system obscures that story, it is wrong.

## Typography

### Display

- Use a serif display face with character and restraint
- Desired qualities: elegant, confident, editorial, high-contrast
- Reserve it for hero lines, core promises, and direction names

### Body

- Use a readable sans-serif for product copy and interface text
- Desired qualities: calm, contemporary, unobtrusive, highly legible

### Mono

- Use monospace only for metadata, labels, and lightweight system framing
- It must never dominate the page

### Typographic Rules

- Let the display face carry identity, not volume
- Use large hero statements with generous line breaks
- Keep interface copy highly readable and slightly denser
- Make metadata clearly secondary without disappearing
- Avoid tiny low-contrast labels on complex surfaces

## Palette

### Core Tones

- Warm paper
- Bone white
- Carbon ink
- Smoked brown-black

### Accent Family

- Ember as the primary warm accent
- Muted brass or champagne for subtle emphasis

### Palette Rules

- Most of the interface should live on light paper surfaces
- Use dark surfaces as anchors, not as the default canvas
- Important text must never sit on muddy mid-tones
- Accent colors should be rare, intentional, and useful
- Surface contrast should create hierarchy before borders try to

## Surface Language

- Large calm panels with clear separation
- Light-first composition with one strong dark anchor where useful
- Thin borders, subtle shadows, and visible tonal steps
- Docked supporting panels rather than tiled equal-weight cards
- Avoid stacking too many half-muted surfaces inside each other

## Layout

- Favor asymmetry with one dominant narrative column
- Give the hero one clear reading path
- Show the product transformation visually, not just verbally
- Supporting content should feel attached to the main story, not scattered below it
- Avoid generic SaaS card grids where everything has the same visual importance

## Motion

- Motion should be slow, intentional, and structural
- Use reveals to support reading order
- Avoid playful or bouncy interactions
- Let transitions feel like panels settling into place

## Content Tone

- Plainly intelligent
- Broadly understandable
- Not design-jargon heavy
- Not mystical, not robotic
- Names for directions should feel relatable and interpretable immediately

## Anti-Patterns

- Generic AI dashboard styling
- Muddy dark heroes with weak contrast
- Too many equal-weight cards
- Tiny metadata that vanishes into the background
- Clever naming that reduces comprehension
- Decorative motion without informational value
- Over-explaining the product in multiple competing boxes
